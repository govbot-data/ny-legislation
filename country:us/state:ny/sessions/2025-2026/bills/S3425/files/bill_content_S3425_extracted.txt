Short title. This act shall be known and may be cited as 2 the "New York health act". 3 § 2. Legislative findings and intent. 1. The state constitution 4 states: "The protection and promotion of the health of the inhabitants 5 of the state are matters of public concern and provision therefor shall 6 be made by the state and by such of its subdivisions and in such manner, 7 and by such means as the legislature shall from time to time determine." 8 (Article XVII, §3.) The legislature finds and declares that all resi- 9 dents of the state have the right to health care. While the federal 10 Affordable Care Act brought many improvements in health care and health 11 coverage, it still leaves many New Yorkers without coverage or with 12 inadequate coverage. Millions of New Yorkers do not get the health care 13 they need or face financial obstacles and hardships to get it. That is 14 not acceptable. There is no plan that has been put forward other than 15 the New York health act that will enable New York state to meet that 16 need. New Yorkers - as individuals, employers, and taxpayers - have 17 experienced a rise in the cost of health care and coverage in recent 18 years, including rising premiums, deductibles and co-pays, restricted 19 provider networks and high out-of-network charges. Many New Yorkers go 20 without health care because they cannot afford it or suffer significant EXPLANATION--Matter in italics (underscored) is new; matter in brackets [ ] is old law to be omitted. Businesses have also experienced 2 increases in the costs of health care benefits for their employees, and 3 many employers are shifting a larger share of the cost of coverage to 4 their employees or dropping coverage entirely. Including long-term 5 services and supports (LTSS) in New York Health is a major step forward 6 for older adults, people with disabilities, and their families. Older 7 adults and people with disabilities often cannot receive the services 8 necessary to stay in the community or other LTSS. Even when older adults 9 and people with disabilities receive LTSS, especially services in the 10 community, it is often at great cost and creates unreasonable demands on 11 unpaid family caregivers, depleting their own or family resources, or 12 impoverishing themselves to qualify for public coverage. Health care 13 providers are also affected by inadequate health coverage in New York 14 state. A large portion of hospitals, health centers and other providers 15 now experience substantial losses due to the provision of care that is 16 uncompensated. Medicaid and Medicare often do not pay rates that are 17 reasonably related to the cost of efficiently providing health care 18 services and sufficient to assure an adequate and accessible supply of 19 health care services, as guaranteed under the New York Health Act. 20 Individuals often find that they are deprived of affordable care and 21 choice because of decisions by health plans guided by the plan's econom- 22 ic interests rather than the individual's health care needs. To address 23 the fiscal crisis facing the health care system and the state and to 24 assure New Yorkers can exercise their right to health care, affordable 25 and comprehensive health coverage must be provided. Pursuant to the 26 state constitution's charge to the legislature to provide for the health 27 of New Yorkers, this legislation is an enactment of state concern for 28 the purpose of establishing a comprehensive universal guaranteed health 29 care coverage program and a health care cost control system for the 30 benefit of all residents of the state of New York. 31 2. (a) It is the intent of the Legislature to create the New York 32 Health program to provide a universal single payer health plan for every 33 resident of the state, funded by broad-based revenue based on ability to 34 pay. The legislature intends that federal waivers and approvals be 35 sought where they will improve the administration of the New York Health 36 program, but the legislature intends that the program be implemented 37 even in the absence of such waivers or approvals. The state shall work 38 to obtain waivers and other approvals relating to Medicaid, Child Health 39 Plus, Medicare, the Basic Health Plan (Essential Plan), the Affordable 40 Care Act, and any other appropriate federal programs, under which feder- 41 al funds and other subsidies that would otherwise be paid to New York 42 State, New Yorkers, and health care providers for health coverage that 43 will be equaled or exceeded by New York Health will be paid by the 44 federal government to New York State and deposited in the New York 45 Health trust fund, or paid to health care providers and individuals in 46 combination with New York Health trust fund payments, and for other 47 program modifications (including elimination of cost sharing and insur- 48 ance premiums). Under such waivers and approvals, health coverage under 49 those programs will, to the maximum extent possible, be replaced and 50 merged into New York Health, which will operate as a true single-payer 51 program. 52 (b) If any necessary waiver or approval is not obtained, the state 53 shall use state plan amendments and seek waivers and approvals to maxi- 54 mize, and make as seamless as possible, the use of federally-subsidized 55 health programs and federal health programs in New York Health. Thus, 56 even where other programs such as Medicaid or Medicare may contribute to S. 3425 3 1 paying for care, it is the goal of this legislation that the coverage 2 will be delivered by New York Health and, as much as possible, the 3 multiple sources of funding will be pooled with other New York Health 4 funds and not be apparent to New York Health members or participating 5 providers. 6 (c) This program will promote movement away from fee-for-service 7 payment, which tends to reward quantity and requires excessive adminis- 8 trative expense, and towards alternate payment methodologies, such as 9 global or capitated payments to providers or health care organizations, 10 that promote quality, efficiency, investment in primary and preventive 11 care, and innovation and integration in the organizing of health care. 12 (d) The program shall promote the use of clinical data to improve the 13 quality of health care and public health, consistent with protection of 14 patient confidentiality. The program shall maximize patient autonomy in 15 choice of health care providers and health care decision making. Care 16 coordination within the program shall ensure management and coordination 17 among a patient's health care services, consistent with patient autonomy 18 and person-centered service planning, rather than acting as a gatekeeper 19 to needed services. 20 (e) The program shall operate with care, skill, prudence, diligence, 21 and professionalism, and for the best interests primarily of the members 22 and health care providers. 23 3. This act does not create or relate to any employment benefit or 24 employment benefit plan, nor does it require, prohibit, or limit the 25 providing of any employment benefit or employment benefit plan. 26 4. In order to promote improved quality of, and access to, health care 27 services and promote improved clinical outcomes, it is the policy of the 28 state to encourage cooperative, collaborative and integrative arrange- 29 ments among health care providers who might otherwise be competitors, 30 under the active supervision of the commissioner of health. It is the 31 intent of the state to supplant competition with such arrangements and 32 regulation only to the extent necessary to accomplish the purposes of 33 this act, and to provide state action immunity under the state and 34 federal antitrust laws to health care providers, particularly with 35 respect to their relations with the single-payer New York Health plan 36 created by this act. 37 5. There have been numerous professional economic analyses of state 38 and national single-payer health proposals, including the New York 39 Health Act, by noted consulting firms and academic economists. They have 40 almost all come to similar conclusions of net savings in the cost of 41 health coverage and health care. These savings are driven by (a) elimi- 42 nating the administrative bureaucracy costs, marketing, and profit of 43 multiple health plans and replacing that with the dramatically lower 44 costs of running a single-payer system; (b) substantially reducing the 45 administrative costs borne by health care providers dealing with those 46 health plans; and (c) using the negotiating power of 20 million consum- 47 ers to achieve lower drug prices. These savings will more than offset 48 costs primarily from (a) relieving patients of deductibles, co-pays, and 49 out-of-network charges; (b) covering the uninsured; (c) increasing 50 provider payment rates above Medicare and Medicaid rates; and (d) 51 replacing uncompensated home health care with paid care. Unlike premiums 52 and out-of-pocket spending, the New York Health Act tax will be progres- 53 sively graduated based on ability to pay. The vast majority of New 54 Yorkers today spend dramatically more in premiums, deductibles and other 55 out-of-pocket costs than they will in New York Health Act taxes. They 56 will have broader coverage (including long-term care), no restricted S. 3425 4 1 provider networks or out-of-network charges, and no deductibles or 2 co-pays. 3 § 3.
