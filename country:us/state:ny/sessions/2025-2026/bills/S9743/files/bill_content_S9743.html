        AN ACT to amend the state finance law, the economic development law  and
          the  environmental conservation law, in relation to the state procure-
          ment process and to healthy and green procurement

          The People of the State of New York, represented in Senate and  Assem-
        bly, do enact as follows:
 
     1    Section  1.  This act shall be known and may be cited as the "New York
     2  state healthy and green procurement act".
     3    § 2. Legislative findings  and  declaration.  The  legislature  hereby
     4  finds and declares that:
     5    (a)  As  New York moves into the twenty-first century the state should
     6  serve as a role model for practices that  will  create  and  maintain  a
     7  healthy  environment  and  vibrant  economy.  The  manufacture,  use and
     8  disposal of commodities and technologies, the construction of  buildings
     9  and the provision of services utilizing toxic chemicals may have adverse
    10  impacts  on  public health and the environment. Persistent, bioaccumula-
    11  tive toxic chemicals, such as mercury, lead, dioxin and poly  brominated
    12  diphenyl  ethers,  are toxic in small amounts, remain in the environment
    13  for long periods of time, and build to dangerous levels in humans,  fish
    14  and  other  animals;  and  this  group of pollutants known as persistent
    15  bioaccumulative toxic chemicals (PBT) pose risks to  public  health  and
    16  the environment through their ability to cause cancer, birth defects and
    17  endocrine  disruption.  Such  chemicals  have polluted hundreds of water
    18  bodies, fish and waterfowl in the state. These  adverse  impacts  impose
    19  costs  on  the  state and, ultimately, society as a whole in the form of
    20  injury, disease and death; health care expenses; disposal, liability and
    21  cleanup costs; the waste of resources and raw materials; and an impaired
    22  natural environment.  PBT and cancer-causing chemicals may be found in a
    23  wide range of consumer products purchased by state  agencies,  including
    24  lighting  supplies,  computers  and  other  office  equipment, vehicles,
    25  medical equipment, building  supplies  and  printing  inks.  Encouraging
    26  innovation,  and  creating  and  choosing  the  safest, most sustainable
    27  commodities, services and technologies will  help  to  ensure  a  higher

     1  quality of life for present and future generations. It will put New York
     2  businesses  in an advantageous position to compete in the global market-
     3  place.
     4    (b)  New  York  looks  forward  to  the time when the state's power is
     5  generated from renewable and clean sources,  when  our  homes,  schools,
     6  businesses   and   government   facilities   are  energy  efficient  and
     7  constructed, refurbished and maintained using healthy and green products
     8  and practices, when pollution prevention is embraced by  government  and
     9  businesses  as  a  way  to  save money and protect public health and the
    10  environment, when government and citizens use energy efficient and clean
    11  vehicles, when pests are controlled with nontoxic or least toxic  alter-
    12  natives,  when  our production of waste is significantly reduced and the
    13  rest is recycled, and when our homes, schools, workplaces, food, air and
    14  water are free from toxic contaminants.
    15    (c) Protecting public health and the environment  is  consistent  with
    16  the  traditional  considerations associated with state procurement prac-
    17  tice, including lowest price, best value, quality, cost and  efficiency.
    18  Determining  quality,  value and efficiency should include the consider-
    19  ation of public  health  and  environmental  impacts.  Considering  such
    20  impacts  early  in  the  procurement  process  and  adopting an ethic of
    21  pollution prevention will not only reduce pollution and waste,  it  will
    22  reduce costs throughout a commodity, service or technology's life cycle.
    23    (d)  Through the volume of government procurement, government can play
    24  a significant role in spurring private sector development of high  value
    25  commodities  and  services.  This,  in  turn,  will  create business and
    26  employment opportunities in  New  York  state,  foster  competition  and
    27  harness  the  energy of the market to produce products and services that
    28  perform better and cost less. As supply increases, prices will decrease,
    29  and high performance, healthy and green commodities, services and  tech-
    30  nologies will become more affordable for all consumers.

    24    § 5. The state finance law is amended by adding a new section 164-a to
    25  read as follows:
    26    § 164-a. Healthy and green procurement.  1. Healthy and green procure-
    27  ment policy. It is hereby declared to be the policy of the state to  the
    28  extent  practicable  to  purchase commodities, services and technologies
    29  that minimize potential adverse impacts on public health and  the  envi-
    30  ronment  when compared with competing commodities, services or technolo-
    31  gies that serve the same purpose.
    32    2. Definitions. For the purposes of this section, the following  terms
    33  shall have the following meanings unless otherwise specified:
    34    a.  "Pollution  prevention" shall mean pollution prevention as defined
    35  in article twenty-eight of the environmental conservation law.
    36    b. "Priority toxic substances of concern" shall include,  but  not  be
    37  limited  to,  any  substance  listed as known to be or reasonably antic-
    38  ipated, pursuant to the National Toxicology Program report  on  carcino-
    39  gens,  to  be  a  human  carcinogen in the national toxicology program's
    40  biennial report on carcinogens submitted to the United  States  Congress
    41  by the secretary of health and human services; and any substance identi-
    42  fied  as  a  persistent  and/or bioaccumulative toxic substance on lists
    43  maintained by the United States environmental protection agency, includ-
    44  ing the national partnership for environmental priorities; or the inter-
    45  national joint commission created pursuant  to  the  Great  Lakes  water
    46  quality  agreement of 1978; and perfluorinated compounds, dioxin, bromi-
    47  nated flame  retardants  and  bisphenol  A  due  to  their  toxicity  in
    48  production, use and disposal.
    49    3. Minimum specifications for healthy and green procurement.  Consist-
    50  ent  with determinations of need required by subdivision five of section
    51  one hundred sixty-three  of  this  article,  all  state  agencies  shall
    52  procure  commodities,  services and technologies that meet or exceed the
    53  following minimum specifications:
    54    a. Recycled content. All copy paper and other paper supplies for which
    55  the United States environmental protection agency has developed recycled
    56  content recommendations pursuant to section  six  thousand  two  of  the

        S. 9743                             4
 
     1  federal resource conservation and recovery act shall be required to meet
     2  or  exceed  the agency's minimum post-consumer material content percent-
     3  ages recommended in the most recent recovered materials advisory  notice
     4  issued  for  such  commodity in the federal register; provided, however,
     5  that xerographic paper shall contain no less than thirty  percent  post-
     6  consumer recycled content. The commissioner shall also make available to
     7  all agencies, one hundred percent post-consumer, processed chlorine-free
     8  copy paper. All agencies shall print publications on recycled paper, and
     9  minimum  percentages shall be met unless costs for such paper exceed the
    10  cost of other available commodities by more than ten percent.
    11    b. Waste reduction. Agencies shall seek to reduce  waste  in  products
    12  and  packaging, including the formulation of policies to promote the use
    13  of double-sided copying and printing to the greatest extent practicable.
    14  Agencies shall favor durability, repairability and reuse when purchasing
    15  supplies. The commissioner shall establish  minimum  specifications  for
    16  waste  reduction  within  twelve  months  of  the effective date of this
    17  section.
    18    c. Energy efficiency. All commodities for  which  the  federal  energy
    19  management  program of the United States department of energy has issued
    20  product energy efficiency recommendations  shall  meet  or  exceed  such
    21  recommendations.    Agencies  shall seek to achieve reductions in energy
    22  and petroleum consumption; adhere to energy star building criteria; seek
    23  out office space and real estate investments in  buildings  with  energy
    24  star  rating; and follow the public service commission's renewable port-
    25  folio standard to increase the purchase of renewable energy so  that  at
    26  least twenty-five percent of the overall annual electric energy require-
    27  ments  of  buildings owned, leased or operated by state agencies will be
    28  renewable energy by two thousand thirty.
    29    d.  Green  buildings.  All  capital   projects   with   an   estimated
    30  construction  cost  of  two  million  dollars  or more involving (1) the
    31  construction of a new building, (2) an addition to an existing building,
    32  or (3) the substantial reconstruction of an existing building  shall  be
    33  designated  and  constructed  to comply with building standards not less
    34  stringent than the standards  prescribed  by  the  United  States  green
    35  building  council  leadership  in energy and environmental design silver
    36  rating and standards set forth in the definition of a "green base build-
    37  ing" as defined in section nineteen of the tax law, or any portion ther-
    38  eof, any modification of or amendments thereto, and to utilize materials
    39  which do not contain polyvinyl chloride to the greatest extent practica-
    40  ble.  In addition, all state-owned and operated buildings of fifty thou-
    41  sand square feet or larger shall be operated to meet such standards  for
    42  existing buildings to the maximum extent practicable that is cost effec-
    43  tive by not later than two thousand twenty-nine.
    44    4.  Specifications  to  incorporate healthy and green procurement.  a.
    45  Beginning one year after the effective date of this section,  all  state
    46  agencies, when procuring commodities, services or technology pursuant to
    47  section  one hundred sixty-three of this article, shall follow practices
    48  and develop solicitation specifications that meet or exceed the  minimum
    49  specifications for healthy and green procurement established in subdivi-
    50  sion three of this section. All such contracts shall include a statement
    51  describing how such minimum specifications were met.
    52    b. In the event that an agency receives no bids or proposals that meet
    53  the  specifications  developed  pursuant to paragraph a of this subdivi-
    54  sion, such agency may withdraw the request for  bids  or  proposals  and
    55  begin  a new procurement with new specifications without such specifica-
    56  tions and award a contract in accordance with other applicable statutes;

     1  provided, however that such agency shall document the reasons  why  such
     2  procurement  does  not  meet  the minimum specifications for healthy and
     3  green procurement  established  in  this  subdivision  and  submit  such
     4  documentation  to  the  commissioner  for inclusion in the annual report
     5  required pursuant to this section and to the office of  the  comptroller
     6  for inclusion in the procurement record.
     7    5. Balancing healthy and green procurement with price, lowest cost and
     8  best value.
     9    a.  Nothing  in  this  section shall be construed as requiring a state
    10  agency to procure a commodity, service or technology that does not  meet
    11  the  form, function and utility required by such agency, or as requiring
    12  a state agency to procure a commodity, service or technology the cost of
    13  which exceeds the cost of an alternative available commodity or  service
    14  by more than ten percent.
    15    b. When determining and comparing costs, state agencies shall consider
    16  cost as defined in section one hundred sixty of this article.
    17    6.  Healthy  and green procurement officer, coordinators, coordinating
    18  council.
    19    a. Within one hundred eighty  days  of  the  effective  date  of  this
    20  section,  the  commissioner shall assign an individual within the office
    21  of general services to serve as the state healthy and green  procurement
    22  officer.    Such officer shall assist the commissioner with carrying out
    23  the commissioner's duties under this section, including but not  limited
    24  to:
    25    (i)  assisting  the commissioner with the development and provision of
    26  guidance for healthy and green procurement pursuant to this section;
    27    (ii) the identification of  at  least  three  "target  categories"  of
    28  commodities,  services  and/or  technologies  annually  pursuant to this
    29  section;
    30    (iii) the development of criteria for the evaluation  of  commodities,
    31  services and/or technologies within targeted categories;
    32    (iv) the creation of approved healthy and green supplies lists;
    33    (v)  the  evaluation of current state agency procurement practices and
    34  the tracking of progress in implementing the healthy and green  procure-
    35  ment  policy,  including  the  integration of environmentally preferable
    36  purchasing and healthy and green supplies lists for all  new  purchasing
    37  of products, services and technologies in target categories;
    38    (vi) the development of the environmental audit program;
    39    (vii) consideration of additional priority toxic substances of concern
    40  as  recommended  by the department of environmental conservation and the
    41  department of health;
    42    (viii) the development of guidelines for public participation;
    43    (ix) the design and implementation of training, outreach and education
    44  programs;
    45    (x) develop metrics for measuring progress in implementing the healthy
    46  and green  procurement  policy,  criteria  and  minimum  specifications,
    47  including  the  identification of milestones and quantifiable goals that
    48  can be used to measure such progress;
    49    (xi) the preparation and submittal of annual reports and the  perform-
    50  ance of a comprehensive five year review; and
    51    (xii)  advise the governor and the legislature regarding the implemen-
    52  tation of the healthy and green procurement policy.
    53    b. Within one hundred eighty  days  of  the  effective  date  of  this
    54  section,  each  state  agency  with  one hundred employees or more shall
    55  assign an individual  within  such  agency  to  serve  as  the  agency's
    56  sustainable procurement coordinator.

     1    7.  Target  commodity,  service  and/or  technology categories. Within

    11  green procurement. Within twelve months of the identification and recom-
    12  mendation of at least three "target categories" pursuant to  subdivision
    13  seven  of this section, the commissioner, in consultation with the state
    14  procurement council shall develop environmentally preferable  purchasing
    15  criteria for the evaluation and procurement of products, services and/or
    16  technologies  within  such  targeted  categories.  The following factors
    17  shall be considered in the development of such criteria:
    18    a. Protection of public health and the environment and  the  conserva-

    20    b.  The  protection  of drinking water, groundwater, surface water and

    22    c. Pollution prevention, including, but not  limited  to,  considering

    25    d. Avoidance of priority toxic substances; and
    26    e. Positive life cycle attributes, including but not  limited  to  the
    27  minimization of potential adverse impacts on public health and the envi-
    28  ronment  associated with raw materials acquisition, production, manufac-
    29  turing, packaging, transportation, distribution, use, operation, mainte-
    30  nance and disposal.
    31    9. Healthy and green supply lists.
    32    a. For each target commodity, service and/or technology category iden-

    40  healthy and green supply list for such category.
    41    b.  The commissioner shall create an approved healthy and green supply
    42  list pursuant to the requirements of the state administrative  procedure

    44  than  twelve  months  following the identification and recommendation of
    45  such category pursuant to subdivision seven of this section,  and  shall
    46  review and revise approved lists annually.
    47    10.  Procurement from healthy and green supply lists.  a. When procur-
    48  ing a commodity, service or technology within a  targeted  category  for
    49  which  an approved healthy and green supply list has been created, state
    50  agencies shall procure such commodity, service or technology  from  such
    51  list.
    52    b.  When a state agency wants to procure a commodity, service or tech-
    53  nology within a targeted category for  which  an  approved  healthy  and
    54  green supply list has been created, but such commodity, service or tech-
    55  nology  does  not  appear on such list, such agency must obtain a waiver
    56  from  the  requirements  of  this  subdivision  from  the  commissioner,

     1  provided,  however,  that  such  waiver  shall  not  be required for the

    15    (ii) disclosed the use and intensity of use for the commodity, service

    19    (iii)  prepared  a  plan  to  investigate alternatives to the selected

    21    c. A state agency may  procure  a  commodity,  service  or  technology
    22  through  a  process  that does not comply with this subdivision when the
    23  purchase of a commodity, service or technology is necessary  to  respond
    24  to  an  emergency which endangers public health or safety, provided such
    25  agency shall within seven business days file a written report  with  the
    26  commissioner  and the office of the comptroller, which shall become part
    27  of the procurement record. The report shall contain the following infor-
    28  mation:
    29    (i) a description of the emergency that prevented compliance with this

    31    (ii) the name of the commodity or technology, or a description of  the
    32  service, its use and intensity of use;
    33    (iii)  a  description  of  the  steps  being taken to safeguard public

    35    (iv) an explanation of how such an emergency can  be  avoided  in  the

    45    b. develop metrics and identification of milestones  and  quantifiable
    46  goals  that  can be used to measure progress in implementing the state's
    47  healthy and green procurement policy.
    48    c. develop an efficient and  practicable  method  for  collecting  and

    51  purchasing trends for commodities, services and technologies,  including

    56  the office of the comptroller, the commissioner, within twelve months of
