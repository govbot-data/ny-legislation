KAVANAGH -- read twice and ordered printed, and when printed to be committed to the Committee on Procurement and Contracts AN ACT to amend the state finance law, the economic development law and the environmental conservation law, in relation to the state procure- ment process and to healthy and green procurement The People of the State of New York, represented in Senate and Assem- bly, do enact as follows: 1 Section 1. This act shall be known and may be cited as the "New York 2 state healthy and green procurement act". 3 § 2. Legislative findings and declaration. The legislature hereby 4 finds and declares that: 5 (a) As New York moves into the twenty-first century the state should 6 serve as a role model for practices that will create and maintain a 7 healthy environment and vibrant economy. The manufacture, use and 8 disposal of commodities and technologies, the construction of buildings 9 and the provision of services utilizing toxic chemicals may have adverse 10 impacts on public health and the environment. Persistent, bioaccumula- 11 tive toxic chemicals, such as mercury, lead, dioxin and poly brominated 12 diphenyl ethers, are toxic in small amounts, remain in the environment 13 for long periods of time, and build to dangerous levels in humans, fish 14 and other animals; and this group of pollutants known as persistent 15 bioaccumulative toxic chemicals (PBT) pose risks to public health and 16 the environment through their ability to cause cancer, birth defects and 17 endocrine disruption. Such chemicals have polluted hundreds of water 18 bodies, fish and waterfowl in the state. These adverse impacts impose 19 costs on the state and, ultimately, society as a whole in the form of 20 injury, disease and death; health care expenses; disposal, liability and 21 cleanup costs; the waste of resources and raw materials; and an impaired 22 natural environment. PBT and cancer-causing chemicals may be found in a 23 wide range of consumer products purchased by state agencies, including 24 lighting supplies, computers and other office equipment, vehicles, 25 medical equipment, building supplies and printing inks. Encouraging 26 innovation, and creating and choosing the safest, most sustainable 27 commodities, services and technologies will help to ensure a higher EXPLANATION--Matter in italics (underscored) is new; matter in brackets [ ] is old law to be omitted. LBD07353-01-5 S. 9743 2 1 quality of life for present and future generations. It will put New York 2 businesses in an advantageous position to compete in the global market- 3 place. 4 (b) New York looks forward to the time when the state's power is 5 generated from renewable and clean sources, when our homes, schools, 6 businesses and government facilities are energy efficient and 7 constructed, refurbished and maintained using healthy and green products 8 and practices, when pollution prevention is embraced by government and 9 businesses as a way to save money and protect public health and the 10 environment, when government and citizens use energy efficient and clean 11 vehicles, when pests are controlled with nontoxic or least toxic alter- 12 natives, when our production of waste is significantly reduced and the 13 rest is recycled, and when our homes, schools, workplaces, food, air and 14 water are free from toxic contaminants. 15 (c) Protecting public health and the environment is consistent with 16 the traditional considerations associated with state procurement prac- 17 tice, including lowest price, best value, quality, cost and efficiency. 18 Determining quality, value and efficiency should include the consider- 19 ation of public health and environmental impacts. Considering such 20 impacts early in the procurement process and adopting an ethic of 21 pollution prevention will not only reduce pollution and waste, it will 22 reduce costs throughout a commodity, service or technology's life cycle. 23 (d) Through the volume of government procurement, government can play 24 a significant role in spurring private sector development of high value 25 commodities and services. This, in turn, will create business and 26 employment opportunities in New York state, foster competition and 27 harness the energy of the market to produce products and services that 28 perform better and cost less. As supply increases, prices will decrease, 29 and high performance, healthy and green commodities, services and tech- 30 nologies will become more affordable for all consumers. 31 § 3. Healthy and green procurement. 1. Healthy and green procure- 27 ment policy. It is hereby declared to be the policy of the state to the 28 extent practicable to purchase commodities, services and technologies 29 that minimize potential adverse impacts on public health and the envi- 30 ronment when compared with competing commodities, services or technolo- 31 gies that serve the same purpose. 32 2. Definitions. For the purposes of this section, the following terms 33 shall have the following meanings unless otherwise specified: 34 a. "Pollution prevention" shall mean pollution prevention as defined 35 in article twenty-eight of the environmental conservation law. 36 b. "Priority toxic substances of concern" shall include, but not be 37 limited to, any substance listed as known to be or reasonably antic- 38 ipated, pursuant to the National Toxicology Program report on carcino- 39 gens, to be a human carcinogen in the national toxicology program's 40 biennial report on carcinogens submitted to the United States Congress 41 by the secretary of health and human services; and any substance identi- 42 fied as a persistent and/or bioaccumulative toxic substance on lists 43 maintained by the United States environmental protection agency, includ- 44 ing the national partnership for environmental priorities; or the inter- 45 national joint commission created pursuant to the Great Lakes water 46 quality agreement of 1978; and perfluorinated compounds, dioxin, bromi- 47 nated flame retardants and bisphenol A due to their toxicity in 48 production, use and disposal. 49 3. Minimum specifications for healthy and green procurement. Consist- 50 ent with determinations of need required by subdivision five of section 51 one hundred sixty-three of this article, all state agencies shall 52 procure commodities, services and technologies that meet or exceed the 53 following minimum specifications: 54 a. Recycled content. All copy paper and other paper supplies for which 55 the United States environmental protection agency has developed recycled 56 content recommendations pursuant to section six thousand two of the S. 9743 4 1 federal resource conservation and recovery act shall be required to meet 2 or exceed the agency's minimum post-consumer material content percent- 3 ages recommended in the most recent recovered materials advisory notice 4 issued for such commodity in the federal register; provided, however, 5 that xerographic paper shall contain no less than thirty percent post- 6 consumer recycled content. The commissioner shall also make available to 7 all agencies, one hundred percent post-consumer, processed chlorine-free 8 copy paper. All agencies shall print publications on recycled paper, and 9 minimum percentages shall be met unless costs for such paper exceed the 10 cost of other available commodities by more than ten percent. 11 b. Waste reduction. Agencies shall seek to reduce waste in products 12 and packaging, including the formulation of policies to promote the use 13 of double-sided copying and printing to the greatest extent practicable. 14 Agencies shall favor durability, repairability and reuse when purchasing 15 supplies. The commissioner shall establish minimum specifications for 16 waste reduction within twelve months of the effective date of this 17 section. 18 c. Energy efficiency. All commodities for which the federal energy 19 management program of the United States department of energy has issued 20 product energy efficiency recommendations shall meet or exceed such 21 recommendations. Agencies shall seek to achieve reductions in energy 22 and petroleum consumption; adhere to energy star building criteria; seek 23 out office space and real estate investments in buildings with energy 24 star rating; and follow the public service commission's renewable port- 25 folio standard to increase the purchase of renewable energy so that at 26 least twenty-five percent of the overall annual electric energy require- 27 ments of buildings owned, leased or operated by state agencies will be 28 renewable energy by two thousand thirty. 29 d. Green buildings. All capital projects with an estimated 30 construction cost of two million dollars or more involving (1) the 31 construction of a new building, (2) an addition to an existing building, 32 or (3) the substantial reconstruction of an existing building shall be 33 designated and constructed to comply with building standards not less 34 stringent than the standards prescribed by the United States green 35 building council leadership in energy and environmental design silver 36 rating and standards set forth in the definition of a "green base build- 37 ing" as defined in section nineteen of the tax law, or any portion ther- 38 eof, any modification of or amendments thereto, and to utilize materials 39 which do not contain polyvinyl chloride to the greatest extent practica- 40 ble. In addition, all state-owned and operated buildings of fifty thou- 41 sand square feet or larger shall be operated to meet such standards for 42 existing buildings to the maximum extent practicable that is cost effec- 43 tive by not later than two thousand twenty-nine. 44 4.
